Seven years in, residents say the NYPD’s Neighborhood Policing program is failing West Harlem

By Victor Swezey

Graphics by Adina Cazacu-De Luca and Kevin Medina

November 15, 2022

Photo by Soleil Guevara

When Eric Adams ran for mayor last year, he put fears of rising crime in New York City at the center of his campaign. Since his election, he has promised to act with “urgency,” moving quickly to increase police presence and bring back the New York Police Department’s controversial plainclothes anti-crime units—rebranded as “Neighborhood Safety Teams.”

Adams’ efforts are the most recent in a series of community-focused policing initiatives that have aimed to reshape the NYPD’s relationship with the city. In 2015, former New York City Mayor Bill de Blasio announced an initiative he deemed similarly innovative: the $170 million “Neighborhood Policing” program. Marking the first expansion of the NYPD in two decades, the program was supposed to “​​bring police and community closer together while keeping crime low.”

But what effect did de Blasio’s signature policing project—and the enormous investment it entailed—actually have on communities? An investigation by Spectator found that in 2022, seven years after the program’s initiation, many residents believe Neighborhood Policing is failing to meet its own objectives in the 30th Precinct. Views of the NYPD in the precinct, which covers parts of West Harlem and Hamilton Heights, remain decidedly negative among most residents.

Over the course of a month, Spectator surveyed 76 residents and small business owners or managers in the 30th Precinct on their familiarity and relationship with police. Just 9 percent of respondents could name one of their neighborhood coordination officers, who are supposed to serve as “liaisons between the police and the community.” Multiple respondents said they only knew the NCOs because of law enforcement encounters such as arrests and traffic violations and reported abuse and harassment by officers in the precinct.

When asked to rank the relationship between the NYPD and the community on a scale of one to five, with one being “very unhealthy” and five being “ideal,” nearly half of the respondents gave the relationship a one. The average score was 2.1.

The NYPD declined to comment on Neighborhood Policing or to make a neighborhood coordination officer available for an interview and did not return requests for comment on Spectator’s findings by the time of publication.

What is Neighborhood Policing for the NYPD and for residents?

The NYPD calls Neighborhood Policing “a comprehensive crime-fighting strategy built on improved communication and collaboration between local police officers and community residents.”

The program’s website claims it “greatly increases” engagement between the NYPD and the communities it polices by dividing each precinct into multiple “sectors” based on the boundaries of existing neighborhoods. Like the classic beat cop, officers are localized to these sectors in order to “increase their familiarity with local residents and local problems,” according to the website. Each sector is anchored by two neighborhood coordination officers, who are supposed to form a bridge between the police and the community, cultivating relationships with local leaders, clergy, and neighborhood institutions.

De Blasio touted the program’s success in the years following its implementation, calling it “the way forward” for a police department that has been plagued by allegations of racial discrimination and excessive force, especially in low-income communities of color.

Neighborhood Policing came to the 30th Precinct in 2017 and was active in all 76 precincts by the following year.

However, contrary to the image presented by de Blasio and the NYPD, West Harlem residents today describe the police force as disengaged and “standoffish.” Community members say officers make little to no effort to get to know them and often take hours to respond to simple calls.

“My impression is that they are showing up, but they are not doing anything,” India Bee, a West Harlem resident, said. “They haven’t actually [taken] the physical action of meeting the people to see what the neighborhood is about.”

Of those surveyed, only 13 percent recognized at least one current NCO when presented with a list of names.

“They barely interact with any of the neighbors or anybody in the community at all,” Karen Carla, who lives in the 30th Precinct, said. “They don’t patrol the neighborhood. They don’t talk. They don’t engage.”

Nearly three-quarters of residents of Community Board 9, which includes the 30th Precinct, are people of color. The community reported a poverty rate of 25.3 percent, almost double the New York City average.

When asked whether they believed the 30th Precinct police force was making an effort to “increas[e] their familiarity with local residents and local problems,” one of the Neighborhood Policing program’s explicit goals, around 80 percent of respondents said “no.”

Adnan Alshabi, the owner of Golden Deli on 137th Street and Broadway, said he does not trust the police to enforce the law, even when he calls them directly in situations involving criminal behavior.

“They never come,” Alshabi said. “I saw [them] a couple times on the corner of 138th [waiting] after 30, 40 minutes … they are not doing good.”

“Who am I beating up now?”

The majority of respondents—even those with relatively favorable views of police—reported that officers were distant and rarely, if ever, interacted with the community. Those who did report interactions with police overwhelmingly said they were for the wrong reasons. Nearly three-quarters of respondents to Spectator’s survey said they had not had a single interaction with the NYPD outside of an emergency situation or formal law enforcement encounter in the last two years. Of the quarter who had had informal interactions with the police, 75 percent reported those interactions to be negative.

One group that felt they were specifically targeted for harassment by officers in the 30th Precinct was app-based delivery drivers.

Yoryi Torres, who delivers for DoorDash and Uber Eats on an electric scooter, said he has received four citations from the NYPD in the last year alone and faces constant harassment.

“If they see you vulnerable, they’ll come and jump on you,” Torres said. “They’ll come like, ‘Give me the bike. Get off your bike. Give me your ID.’”

Ariel, a DoorDash driver who spoke on the condition of anonymity for fear of reprisal, said he was recently beaten by multiple officers during an arrest.

“They all come in a manner of like, ‘Who am I beating up now?’” Ariel said. “They’re worse than a gang.”

Several community members said that the NYPD had used “stop-and-frisk” approaches in the neighborhood, a practice with a deeply controversial history. The stop-and-frisk policing method involves officers briefly detaining and searching anyone on the street based on “reasonable suspicion” of criminal activity, a lower level of scrutiny than the typical legal standard of “probable cause.”

The policy was ruled unconstitutional in federal court in 2013 after data revealed that New Yorkers of color were stopped at far higher rates than their white counterparts. However, advocates suspect that the practice continues today.

“There is no doubt in my mind that stop-and-frisk still happens, very much more discreetly,” Isaac Scott, an East Harlem-based human rights activist and fellow at Columbia’s Center for Justice, said. “It’s a tactic that has been embedded into the NYPD’s strategy.”

“The Dirty 30”

Many activists and community members cite mutual distrust, which they say is created by years of aggressive and discriminatory policing tactics, as a major obstacle to the kind of community-focused relationship that Neighborhood Policing is supposed to foster.

New York City Councilwoman Kristin Richardson Jordan, whose district includes parts of the 30th Precinct, said she has had reservations about Neighborhood Policing since she first learned about the program.

“I was not a big fan of this program from its inception,” Richardson Jordan said. “It reminded me of the plainclothes unit officer, which patrolled the neighborhood, and caused great harm, and actually had a few cases of police abuse.”

De Blasio disbanded the NYPD’s plainclothes unit in 2020, at the height of the Black Lives Matter protests that followed the murder of George Floyd by a police officer. The units, which wear civilian clothes while on duty, have been criticized for carrying out the majority of stop-and-frisk arrests. They have also been accused of racial profiling and using excessive force, most notably in the 1999 murder of Amadou Diallo, a Guinean immigrant shot 19 times after reaching for his wallet.

Many community members cite this confrontational approach to policing as a major reason for their hesitance to trust the police.

“The way that we were raised in this community, you just don’t go to the cops,” Bee said. “911 is a joke to us.”

Other residents referred to the precinct’s reputation as “The Dirty 30.” The nickname derives from a major corruption scandal that rocked the precinct from 1992 to 1995, resulting in the arrests of 33 officers for crimes ranging from perjury to extortion to grand larceny.

Scott said the community’s distrust interferes with the police’s ability to enforce the law.

“There’s a way in which the NYPD uses people in a hierarchical way that makes you believe that it’s not about the crime and public safety, but it’s about the numbers,” Scott said, referring to allegations that the NYPD uses a discriminatory quota system to incentivize arrests in low-income communities of color. “Folks don’t trust them with information.”

The only official academic study of the NYPD’s Neighborhood Policing program, conducted by professors at the University of Colorado and the University of Florida in 2020, found that the program had a negligible impact on the police’s ability to fight crime.

“They can only do so much”

Despite the precinct’s troubled history, some in the community still retain a positive attitude toward police.

For police supporters, policymakers who refuse to crack down on criminals are to blame for the neighborhood’s rising crime rates. In their eyes, the police are simply trying to enforce the law under suboptimal conditions.

“​​I have to commend the police because a lot of times their hands are tied,” Tindu Menninger, an attendee at the 30th Precinct’s Oct. 27 Community Council meeting, said. “These people get arrested on Monday, and in three to four hours get back on the street. … It’s an administration problem.”

Precinct community councils, which have existed since the mid-20th century, hold monthly public meetings and are intended as a forum for the community to offer feedback and advice to the police. These organizations were folded into the Neighborhood Police program following its implementation.

However, community councils have historically been criticized for their pro-police bias and unwillingness to hold the NYPD accountable. A spokesperson for the group Communities United for Police Reform said the councils “are heavily NYPD-influenced and rarely include police critics” in a 2021 interview with the New York Daily News.

Even some at the meeting felt pessimistic about the council’s odds of having any meaningful impact on precinct policy.

“Honestly, they’re not going to do nothing,” Menninger’s sister Deborah Elliot said.

Elliot said she was attending a Community Council meeting for the first time at her sister’s invitation. She had come to report a lack of lighting around her building that made her feel unsafe on her early-morning commute.

Joyce Adewumi, president of the 30th Precinct Community Council, emphasized the need for reconciliation between the police and the community.

“We can’t just leave everything onto the police,” Adewumi said. “What about us? What can we do? What are we doing that’s contributing to the problem?”

Adewumi herself could not name a single NCO in the 30th Precinct.

Falling arrests, differing perspectives

Regardless of their attitudes toward police, many West Harlem residents said that the year 2020 ushered in a significant shift in the relationship between the precinct and the community.

Between social distancing measures imposed as a result of the COVID-19 pandemic and police backlash against protests for institutional reform after the murder of George Floyd, residents say police presence in the community has been visibly reduced.

“Ever since the whole thing with the defunding the police, it’s like, poof, you don’t see them,” Ronnie Johns, who lives in Hamilton Heights, said.

Though the pandemic may have been associated with a visible decrease in officers on the street, arrests for low-level crimes like trespassing, drug possession, and weapon possession in the 30th Precinct actually began decreasing almost immediately after the implementation of Neighborhood Policing.

According to the same study from the University of Florida and the University of Colorado, misdemeanor arrests and “proactive” arrests—those not precipitated by a 911 call—fell significantly in the 30th Precinct after the implementation of Neighborhood Policing in 2017.

Not all stakeholders feel the same about these trends.

Robert Gangi, the executive director of the Police Reform Organizing Project, compared decreasing arrests in communities of color to “[putting] a knife in my back … nine inches, and you pull it out three inches,” saying he prefers if there are “fewer arrests of any kind.”

On the other hand, many community members—even those with deeply negative attitudes toward police—still feel that their presence is necessary to stop crime and ensure public safety.

“It’s impossible for there to be no presence and for it to be a positive,” Johns said. “That gives everybody that wants to do something wrong the green light.”

Though Scott says he supports police abolition, he believes these immediate concerns about security must be taken seriously.

“Where we’re at right now as a society, it would be pandemonium if we didn’t have the police,” Scott said. “There’s a lot that has to go into this shift toward more community-based solutions.”

“It’s hard for the police to police the police”

While residents of the 30th Precinct largely agree with the need for policing, those surveyed were overwhelmingly unhappy with the NYPD’s current presence in their community. Amid this historically tense dynamic, Spectator’s investigation has found that residents do not believe that the Neighborhood Policing program is succeeding in its promise to “bring police and community closer together,” while one study indicates that it has also faced difficulties with its goal of “keeping crime low.”

Given these ongoing concerns about the efficacy of the NYPD’s reform efforts, activists are increasingly considering the possibility of police democratization as a solution.

In recent years, the movement to implement an elected civilian review board has gained significant momentum. Delegates would represent every neighborhood in the city, and the board would be vested with the ability to initiate investigations into allegations of misconduct and make binding decisions. The ECRB would also work in conjunction with an independent special prosecutor who would handle criminal cases filed against police officers.

By contrast, members of the existing Civilian Complaint Review Board are appointed by various city agencies, including the NYPD. Furthermore, the board’s decisions essentially function as recommendations, with the police commissioner making the final decision as to whether officers face disciplinary action.

Richardson Jordan said she is currently working on sponsoring legislation to bring the issue of an elected civilian review board to the New York City Council.

When asked about the proposal, many residents of the 30th Precinct expressed their support.

“It’s hard for the police to police the police because of the brotherhood,” Rodney Woodard, who lives in Hamilton Heights, said. “You need somebody who’s going to be objective.”

For activists like Richardson Jordan, this is the fundamental problem that unites frustration over the failures of de Blasio’s Neighborhood Policing with worries about potential abuses under Adams’ Neighborhood Safety Teams. Both programs were imposed from the top down by the mayor’s office, and both leave little room for genuine democratic participation by the communities they impact.

“Without that accountability, you simply have those with unchecked power patrolling the streets in greater force and greater number,” Richardson Jordan said. “It’s very hard to build a rapport or any sort of meaningful relationship with the community when there’s no way for the community to truly hold the local police accountable.”

While these democratic reforms are gaining traction, they’re still far from implementation. In the meantime, residents say officers merely acting on the promises set out by the Neighborhood Policing program could begin the work of repairing the fraught relationship between the police and the community in the 30th Precinct.

“They should be introducing themselves. They should be standing on the corner … [saying,] ‘We’re here for you if you need us,’” Bee said.
